Introduction

Welcome to "How does cinema view the world?", a data visualization journey through the cinematic world. In this project we will explore how cinema portrays the world and how this has changed over time.

We aim to uncover the biases with respect to the portrayal of countries, cities, and characters in movies. We will also look at how these biases have changed over time and how they differ between countries.


Large scale analysis

Before delving deep into the analysis we must first take a good look at the data. The data we used is the CMU Movie Summary Corpus, which is a collection of 42,306 movie summaries. We have expanded this dataset by using LLMs(OpenAI GPT-3.5-Turbo model) to generate the locations depicted in the story and the characters alignment but also nationality.



Our preprocessing only identified 21,000 movies with a country present and around 13,000 with a city present. Even with the lower numbers we still have a lot of data to work with.



Unsurprisingly, the distribution of movies in countries is not uniform. Most of the movies are from the United States, followed by the United Kingdom and India. This will be important to keep in mind when analyzing the data and to account for the bias that might be present.



Going into vector space

In order to be able to easily extract meaning from movie summaries a common technique is to use embeddings. Embeddings are a way to represent pieces of text as vectors, where meaning is encoded in the distance between vectors. We used the OpenAI Ada2 model to generate embeddings for our movie summaries.

We have done this for the movie summaries in order to be able to quantify the similarity between movies and to extract clusters of movies. We can then use these clusters to see how the movies are distributed across countries and notice shifts in the distribution over time and similarities between countries.

For a global view of the embeddings we used T-SNE to reduce the dimensionality of the embeddings to 2 dimensions which allows us to easily visualize the embeddings. By playing around with the search terms we can see that the embeddings do capture some meaning and some clusters can be identified.

We are even able to observe some clusters for specific movie countries. Some particular countries are interesting to look at. For example, for Germany we can see a cluster that is very close to the cluster of war movies from before and France has a close cluster to movies about Love but also a cluster of movies about war.

We can also observe that countries such as the United States and the United Kingdom have no clear clusters and are more spread out across the plot showing that they are portrayed in many different ways as opposed to other countries such as Vietnam where most movies are concentrated in a single cluster.
